Percy Bysshe Shelley — 1818

Ozymandias

Power Legacy Time Nature's indifference Hubris
Context
Shelley was a radical Romantic poet who actively opposed tyranny and monarchy. Written in 1818, when Napoleon's empire had recently collapsed — a direct contemporary parallel. Ancient Egypt was fashionable after Napoleon's 1798 invasion, making the setting resonant. The name "Ozymandias" is a Greek transliteration of the Egyptian pharaoh Ramesses II. Written as a competition with his friend Horace Smith, who wrote a lesser-known poem on the same topic.

Summary

A first-person speaker reports what a traveller told them: in the desert stands a ruined statue of a great king. The inscription on the pedestal boasts of supreme power, yet nothing remains around it — only empty sand.

Form & Structure

Sonnet (14 lines) — a form traditionally associated with power, love, and control. Shelley subverts this, using the form to undermine the idea of lasting power. The irregular rhyme scheme (ABABACDCEDEFEF) mirrors the crumbling, disordered remains of the statue — order is breaking down. There is a triple narrative distance: the speaker reports the traveller who reports the sculptor who preserved the king's expression. Ozymandias's voice only reaches us through others — already diminished. The volta ("Nothing beside remains") arrives with devastating simplicity after the king's grandiose speech.

Key Quotes & Analysis

"Half sunk, a shattered visage lies"
The statue is incomplete — the word "half" implies ongoing decay. "Shattered visage" fragments the face of power literally and symbolically; a ruler's face was the ultimate symbol of their authority. "Lies" carries a double meaning: the statue lies on the ground, but the inscription it carries is also a lie.
"sneer of cold command"
The alliteration of hard 'c' sounds mirrors the clipped cruelty of the king. "Cold" suggests both emotional detachment and the lifeless stone itself. The paradox is that the sculptor's "mocking" hand has outlasted the king — art preserves, but also satirises power. The sneer belongs to the tyrant, but it is the sculptor who gets the last laugh.
"My name is Ozymandias, King of Kings"
The biblical echo — "King of Kings" is a title for Jesus in Revelation — is an act of extraordinary hubris. The direct speech gives the king a voice only to immediately undercut it. The first-person "My" is possessive and domineering; by the poem's end, possession over anything has become absurd.
"Look on my Works, ye Mighty, and despair!"
An imperative addressed to other rulers, intended as a boast of supremacy. The exclamation mark conveys Ozymandias's certainty. The crushing irony is that the poem's reader has already seen there is nothing left to look on — the instruction despairingly answers itself. "Despair" is the poem's emotional verdict on human ambition.
"Nothing beside remains"
Three monosyllabic words after the grandiose boast. The effect is devastating in its simplicity — Shelley uses minimal language to deliver maximum irony. "Nothing" is the logical opposite of the "Works" Ozymandias invites us to admire. The full stop enforces finality.
"The lone and level sands stretch far away"
The sibilance creates a slow, vast, hissing emptiness. Alliteration of 'l' in "lone and level" slows the line to a mournful pace. The landscape is indifferent — nature does not mourn the fallen empire, it simply continues. The final image is of infinite, uncaring space, making the statue's pretensions seem microscopic.

⬆ Grade 9 Angle

The triple narrative voice enacts the poem's argument. Ozymandias's words survive only because a sculptor preserved them, and the sculptor's work survives only because a traveller reported it, and the traveller's account survives only because a poet recorded it. It is art — not power — that endures. The poem is its own refutation of Ozymandias's claims.

vs London — Blake
Power & oppressionPolitical angerIrony
▼
Similarities

Both poems are furious indictments of institutional power, and both use irony to do it. Shelley lets Ozymandias condemn himself through his own boast; Blake lets the city condemn itself through the accumulation of suffering its institutions produce. In both poems, the powerful are shown as the source of others' misery — and both poets position themselves as witnesses from a distance, observing rather than participating in the system they critique. Both also frame power as something that marks or damages the human body: Blake's "marks of weakness, marks of woe" are inscribed on faces, while Shelley's tyrant wears a "sneer of cold command" — power made physical.

Key Difference

Blake's anger is despairing — his "mind-forg'd manacles" are internalised, suggesting London's citizens have been conditioned to accept their own oppression. There is no escape in Blake's poem; the suffering is ongoing and the poem ends in a "blights with plagues" — perpetual damage. Shelley is ultimately more hopeful: Ozymandias's power has already been destroyed by time. For Blake, oppressive power is active and present; for Shelley, it is already dead. This is a crucial contrast: Blake sees no end to the suffering, Shelley shows the powerful's defeat as inevitable — just slow.

Essay Point

Both Blake and Shelley use irony as their primary weapon against power, but to different effect. Blake's irony is bitter and accumulative — the "chartered Thames" should evoke freedom but only marks ownership. Shelley's irony is singular and annihilating — a single inscription undoes centuries of imperial pretension. The difference in scale reflects the difference in their arguments: Blake sees oppression as systemic, Shelley sees it as a single ruler's folly.

vs My Last Duchess — Browning
Arrogance & controlArt as powerDramatic irony
▼
Similarities

Both poems feature powerful men who are undermined by their own words — a technique of dramatic irony so complete that neither speaker seems to realise how damning their monologue is. Both use art as a vehicle for exploring control: the Duke controls his wife through a portrait, Ozymandias tries to control his legacy through a statue. Both men use the second person to address audiences they expect to be impressed — the Duke addresses a marriage negotiator, Ozymandias addresses "ye Mighty" — and both expect submission. Both reveal a profound link between ego and cruelty.

Key Difference

The Duke's power in the poem is still fully operational — he has had his wife killed, he is arranging another marriage, and his control of the portrait means he controls who sees it and how. Ozymandias's power is entirely extinct: "Nothing beside remains." This is the critical structural difference — Browning shows tyranny in action, Shelley shows tyranny already defeated. In terms of what they ask the reader to feel: Browning produces horror at present cruelty; Shelley produces a kind of grim satisfaction at power's inevitable end.

Essay Point

Both Browning and Shelley use art as a metaphor for power's attempt to control its own legacy. The Duke's painting and Ozymandias's statue are both intended to project authority — but both backfire. The sculptor in Ozymandias reads the king's "passions" accurately enough to preserve them — and in doing so, immortalises not greatness but cruelty. Similarly, the Duke's portrait preserves not his authority but his possessiveness. In both cases, the artwork outlasts the man's intentions.

vs Storm on the Island — Heaney
Human constructs vs natureFalse securityPolitical allegory
▼
Similarities

Both poems show human structures — physical, political, imperial — overwhelmed by a force that is indifferent to them. In Ozymandias, the desert reduces the greatest empire to rubble: "the lone and level sands stretch far away." In Storm on the Island, the storm reduces the islanders' careful preparations to nothing — "we are bombarded by the empty air." Both poems use the landscape as an argument: the vastness of the desert and the force of the storm dwarf human certainty. Both also have political readings beyond the literal: Heaney is widely read as writing about Northern Ireland, Shelley about the folly of political tyranny in any era.

Key Difference

In Ozymandias, the ruin is already complete — the poem looks back at destruction already done. In Storm on the Island, the storm is happening now, in real time. Heaney's use of the present tense creates immediate, ongoing threat; Shelley's past-tense report creates a kind of archaeological distance. This difference in tense changes the emotional register: Shelley produces ironic satisfaction at the fallen tyrant, Heaney produces existential dread at an ongoing assault. Heaney also has a collective speaker ("we"), emphasising community under threat; Shelley's individual ruler stands alone in the sand.

Essay Point

Both Heaney and Shelley use the natural world to expose the illusion of human certainty. Heaney's islanders have "wizened earth," "no natural shelter," and hay that is "no use" — every preparation fails. Shelley's Ozymandias built the greatest monument he could and it amounts to "Nothing beside remains." Both poets argue that belief in permanence is a form of arrogance that the natural world will always, eventually, correct.

vs Tissue — Dharker
Fragility of powerRecords & legacyWhat endures
▼
Similarities

Both poems meditate on the impermanence of human power and the fragility of the records we use to assert it. Dharker uses paper — maps, deeds, receipts — as a metaphor for the flimsy material on which empires and systems of ownership are founded. Shelley uses a ruined stone monument. Both suggest that power's monuments are more fragile than those who built them believed: Ozymandias's "colossal wreck" and Dharker's paper that "can be torn" reach the same conclusion through different materials. Both also carry a quiet implication that the natural world (light through paper, desert sands) will always outlast human construction.

Key Difference

Dharker's tone is notably more hopeful than Shelley's. Where Ozymandias is an ironic takedown — the poem savours the destruction of the tyrant — Tissue seems to celebrate the fragility of power structures. Dharker's paper "lets the light / shine through," suggesting that when the records of ownership and control are dissolved, something luminous becomes possible. Shelley's poem ends in emptiness; Dharker's ends in possibility. The difference reflects their contexts: Shelley is attacking specific political tyranny, Dharker is imagining a world freed from ownership and borders.

Essay Point

Dharker and Shelley both use the physical material of power's legacy — paper and stone — to expose its fragility. The irony in Ozymandias is that stone, the hardest, most permanent of materials, has not lasted. Dharker's insight is similar but applied to paper: the most fragile material is also used to record what people believe is most permanent (ownership, identity, money). Both poets argue that what power believes to be its most durable expressions are in fact the most vulnerable.

vs Checking Out Me History — Agard
Control of narrativeWho writes historyPower's legacy
▼
Similarities

Both poems are about the relationship between power and historical narrative — specifically, who gets to control what is remembered. Ozymandias wanted to control his legacy absolutely ("Look on my Works, ye Mighty") but failed; Agard's poem deals with a colonial educational power that successfully controlled which histories were taught for generations. Both show that power over narrative is central to political power more broadly. Both poems also have a note of resistance: Shelley's irony undermines Ozymandias's intended message, just as Agard's Creole dialect undermines the authority of standard English.

Key Difference

Ozymandias's erasure from history was involuntary — time and nature undid him. The erasure in Checking Out Me History was deliberate: "Dem tell me / Wha dem want to tell me" — someone actively chose which histories to teach. This makes Agard's poem more directly political: the oppression is not an accident of time but an act of will. Furthermore, Agard's speaker is actively reclaiming what was taken ("carving out me identity"); Ozymandias has no one to reclaim his legacy — there is simply nothing left.

Essay Point

Both poems ask: who controls what history remembers? Shelley's answer is that time is the ultimate arbiter — even the most powerful rulers are erased. Agard's answer is more urgent: certain histories were not erased by time but by deliberate political choice. Shelley can afford ironic detachment because the tyrant is already gone; Agard cannot, because the erasure he describes is recent and its effects are still being lived. The contrast in tone — Shelley's cool irony vs Agard's angry anaphora — reflects this difference in urgency.

John Agard — 1996

Checking Out Me History

Identity Cultural oppression Colonialism Knowledge & power Resistance
Context
John Agard was born in British Guiana (now Guyana) in 1949 and moved to Britain in 1977. He was educated under a colonial British system that taught British history while largely ignoring Caribbean, African, and South American history. The poem directly attacks this educational erasure. Writing in Creole dialect is itself a political act — Agard refuses to use the coloniser's standard English. The historical figures he names — Toussaint L'Ouverture (who led the Haitian slave revolution), Nanny of the Maroons (Jamaican freedom fighter), and Mary Seacole (Caribbean-British nurse in the Crimean War) — were largely absent from British school curricula for decades.

Summary

The speaker angrily catalogues the trivial British history forced on him at school — Dick Whittington, Guy Fawkes, 1066 — and contrasts this with the powerful Caribbean and African historical figures he had to discover for himself. He insists that knowing his own history is essential to forming his identity.

Form & Structure

Free verse with Creole dialect — the rejection of formal, standard English is itself a resistance to colonial authority. The poem has two visually distinct sections: the indented, italicised stanzas for the "real" historical figures are set apart as sacred, elevated — almost reverential. The block stanzas for colonial history are dense and dismissive. Anaphora ("Dem tell me / Dem tell me") creates a drumbeat of indoctrination that builds in oppressive weight. Short, isolated lines (particularly in the Toussaint section) give each word maximum impact. The poem ends with an active, ongoing verb — "carving out me identity" — suggesting the process is not yet finished.

Key Quotes & Analysis

"Dem tell me / Dem tell me / Wha dem want to tell me"
The triple anaphora creates a suffocating, repetitive pressure — this is how ideology works, through relentless repetition. "Dem" (they) is deliberately vague — it encompasses the colonial education system, the British state, authority in general. The Creole syntax is not a mistake; it is a deliberate refusal to use the oppressor's language on its own terms.
"Bandage up me eye with me own history"
A powerful, disturbing metaphor: the colonial education system has blinded the speaker using his own people's history as the instrument. "Bandage" suggests both a wound (the history itself is violence) and a medical covering (the truth is being hidden for the speaker's supposed "good"). The possessive "me own" is particularly painful — what should be his inheritance is weaponised against him.
"Toussaint / a slave / with vision"
The extreme fragmentation into single-word or two-word lines gives each element equal weight and forces the reader to pause. The juxtaposition of "slave" and "vision" is an oxymoron that challenges the reader's assumptions: the two words should not coexist, yet they do. This formal technique mirrors the poem's argument — history has erased the complexity of these figures, and Agard restores it.
"a toussaint de hurricane / de beacon"
The twin metaphors work in tension: a hurricane is violent, destructive, unstoppable — but a beacon guides, offers hope and direction. Toussaint L'Ouverture was both: he led a violent revolution and lit the way for Caribbean independence. Agard refuses to simplify the figure into a comfortable hero. The lowercase "de" (instead of "the") keeps the dialect authentic.
"carving out me identity"
The poem's culminating image. "Carving" is active, effortful, ongoing — not a passive receiving of identity but a deliberate construction of it. It also implies resistance: carving against something hard. The present participle ("carving") rather than past tense means this work is unfinished — the poem doesn't offer a neat resolution, but an ongoing act of self-determination.

  1. "Dem tell me / Dem tell me / Wha dem want to tell me"anaphora, indoctrination, repetition as oppression
  2. "Bandage up me eye with me own history"metaphor of deliberate blinding; "me own" — painful possession
  3. "Toussaint / a slave / with vision"fragmented lines, oxymoron, restoring complexity
  4. "a toussaint de hurricane / de beacon"dual metaphor — violence and hope, not simplified
  5. "carving out me identity"active verb, ongoing, resistance, unfinished
⬆ Grade 9 Angle

The dialect is the argument. Agard is not writing in Creole for accessibility or character — he is making the point that the poem itself, in its form and language, is an act of the reclamation it describes. Form and content are inseparable. A student who notices this will immediately be writing at a higher level than one who treats the dialect as a stylistic quirk.

vs London — Blake
Political angerPsychological captivityVoice as resistance
▼
Similarities

Both are furious political poems in which the speaker witnesses institutional power oppressing ordinary people and demands to be heard. Both use imagery of psychological captivity: Blake's "mind-forg'd manacles" and Agard's "bandage up me eye" both suggest oppression that has been internalised, made invisible. Both poets are also resistant in their form: Blake's ABAB hymn structure is a deliberate use of familiar, communal form to deliver radical content; Agard's Creole is a rejection of the coloniser's linguistic authority. In both cases, the poem's language is itself an act of defiance.

Key Difference

The crucial difference is in the direction of travel. Blake's poem ends in defeat and perpetual damage — the "youthful Harlot's curse" blights the newborn, suggesting oppression is self-perpetuating with no end in sight. Agard ends in active, ongoing resistance: "carving out me identity" — the present participle insists the work is being done. Blake's speaker watches suffering he cannot stop; Agard's speaker is actively reclaiming what was taken. This makes Agard's poem ultimately more hopeful, though both are equally angry.

Essay Point

Both Blake and Agard use the act of looking as a central metaphor for oppression. Blake "wanders" through London and sees the marks of suffering on every face. Agard's speaker has been deliberately prevented from looking — his eyes were bandaged with his own history. The contrast is significant: Blake sees too much and is powerless; Agard was forced to see too little and fights back. Both poems argue that what people are allowed to see is a form of political control.

vs The Emigrée — Rumens
Identity under threatCultural rootsPreservation vs reclamation
▼
Similarities

Both poems explore how identity is constructed in relation to cultural and geographical roots that external forces have tried to sever or erase. Rumens's speaker holds onto a "child's vocabulary" of her home city even though she can no longer return — the memory is almost physical in its persistence. Agard's speaker clings to historical figures — Toussaint, Nanny — that the colonial education system tried to erase. Both speakers refuse to allow external forces to define who they are, and both find that the roots of identity, once established, cannot be fully destroyed.

Key Difference

Rumens's poem is elegiac — it mourns what has been lost and preserves memory as a private act of survival. The speaker's city "may be at war, it may be sick with tyrants" but her memory of it is untouched. It is a deeply personal, inward resistance. Agard's resistance is outward and combative — he demands public recognition of suppressed histories, not just private memory. Rumens preserves; Agard reclaims. The emotional register differs too: Rumens is mournful and wistful, Agard is angry and rhythmically assertive.

Essay Point

Both Rumens and Agard write about identities that have been targeted by political power, but their strategies of resistance differ fundamentally. Rumens's speaker holds on to what she has — memory is her fortress. Agard's speaker does not have the luxury of memory, because the colonial system stripped him of his history before he could form it. He must go looking — actively "carving out" rather than protecting. This difference reflects the nature of the violence done to each speaker: displacement versus erasure.

vs Ozymandias — Shelley
Who controls historyPower over narrativeLegacy
▼
Similarities

Both poems centre on the question of who controls historical narrative. Ozymandias believed he could dictate his own legacy — "Look on my Works, ye Mighty" — but was undone by time. Agard confronts a colonial system that successfully dictated which histories were taught: "Dem tell me / Wha dem want to tell me." In both poems, power's relationship with history is shown to be a form of control — and both poems resist that control, one through irony, one through direct confrontation. Both also suggest that the historical record is always partial, shaped by those with authority.

Key Difference

The oppressor in Ozymandias has already been defeated by time — the statue is in ruins, the empire is gone. Agard's oppressor (British colonial education) is recent, and its effects are still being felt in the speaker's sense of identity. Shelley can therefore be ironic and detached; Agard must be urgent and angry. Moreover, Ozymandias was erased involuntarily — time did it to him. The figures Agard celebrates were erased deliberately by political choice, which makes the injustice more acute.

Essay Point

Both poems implicitly ask: what survives? Shelley's answer is devastating — almost nothing. Agard's poem suggests the answer is more complex: the histories survive, but buried, requiring active excavation. The difference in the poets' stances — Shelley's cool wit versus Agard's rhythmic fury — reflects the difference between observing power's failure from a position of safety, and living inside the damage that power caused.

Jane Weir — 2009

Poppies

Grief & loss War's human cost Memory Suppressed emotion Family
Context
Jane Weir wrote this poem in 2009 for Carol Ann Duffy's anthology "The Not Dead," which collected poetry based on interviews with veterans suffering from PTSD. While most poems in the anthology took the soldier's perspective, Weir chose to write from the viewpoint of a mother. The poem is intentionally not tied to a specific war — the poppies of Remembrance Sunday connect it to WWI but also to all modern conflict. Weir's poem is notable for its almost total absence of the word "war" — the violence is never named directly, only felt through domestic detail.

Summary

A mother recalls the moment before her son left — possibly for war. She pinned a poppy on his lapel, smoothed his collar, suppressed her grief. The poem ends with her standing at a war memorial, listening for the sound of his voice on the wind. The question of whether he is alive or dead is deliberately left unresolved.

Form & Structure

Free verse — the lack of fixed form mirrors the mother's emotional fragility and lack of control. Long, enjambed sentences spill over lines as if the grief cannot be contained — thoughts run on the way anxiety does. The poem is non-linear, shifting between the memory of his departure and the present at the memorial. Domestic imagery (lapel pins, blazer collars, Sellotape) is juxtaposed with military imagery (war memorial, poppies) — the home front and the battlefield overlap without ever being named as such. The poem ends with no resolution, mid-action, which enacts the mother's permanent state of waiting.

Key Quotes & Analysis

"Before you left, I pinned one onto your lapel"
The domestic intimacy of "pinned" — a mother dressing her child — is overlaid with the weight of the poppy symbol. The poppy is worn in November to remember the war dead: by pinning it on him before he leaves, the mother is symbolically preparing him for death, though this is a thought she cannot consciously hold. The tender action and the terrible resonance operate simultaneously.
"steeled the softening"
The verb "steeled" has a military connotation — to steel oneself is a soldier's phrase — but here it is a mother using it against her own emotion. "Softening" is grief beginning to break through; she physically suppresses it. The alliterative 's' sounds link the two words into a single act of internal conflict. The compressed phrase does a remarkable amount of work: it shows us the effort of controlling grief without ever naming it.
"released a song of loss"
A moment of emotional breakthrough — the restraint gives way. "Released" implies something that had been imprisoned; the suppressed grief escapes. "Song of loss" is a synaesthetic image — emotion expressed as sound — and the word "song" carries both beauty and mourning (like a lament or a dirge). The line is placed at a moment of physical separation (she lets go of the door), so the release is literal as well as emotional.
"leaned against it like a wishbone"
The simile of the wishbone is extraordinarily compressed. A wishbone is fragile — it breaks. It is associated with luck and with wishes, both of which are uncertain. The image captures the mother's hope (she is wishing for his return) and her fragility (she might break). The act of leaning on the door suggests both physical weakness and the desperate desire to stay connected to him through the barrier.
"hoping to hear / your playground voice catch on the wind"
"Playground voice" regresses the son to childhood — the mother's grief is also a grief for the child she once had, not just the soldier she might lose. "Catch on the wind" suggests something fleeting, almost present but not quite — the voice is just out of reach. This is a perfect image of grief: you almost hear it, almost see it, but it slips away. The enjambment across "hear / your" enacts that reaching, catching quality.

⬆ Grade 9 Angle

The poem is about suppressed grief — the word "war" never appears; the son's fate is never confirmed. Grade 9 analysis focuses on what is absent as much as what is present. The mother never says she is afraid or devastated — but every domestic detail encodes these feelings. The highest-level insight is that the poem's form (fragmented, enjambed, unresolved) mirrors the mother's psychological state.

vs Remains — Armitage
Psychological aftermathSuppressed emotionInability to move on
▼
Similarities

Both poems are about the psychological aftermath of war — the fact that the damage continues long after the conflict itself. Neither poem describes battle directly; both focus on what is left behind in the mind. Both also use indirect language to convey extreme emotion: Weir encodes her mother's grief in domestic details ("steeled the softening"), and Armitage encodes the soldier's trauma in casual colloquial language ("probably armed, possibly not"). In both cases, the emotional intensity is produced by the gap between the register of the language and the weight of what is being described. Both poems also resist resolution — neither ends with healing or closure.

Key Difference

The most important contrast is perspective: Poppies gives us the view from the home front — a mother's grief at the edge of loss — while Remains gives us the soldier's first-person account of guilt. Weir's mother is entirely passive, waiting and listening for a voice that may never come: "hoping to hear / your playground voice catch on the wind." Armitage's soldier is haunted by something he did, not something done to him. The emotional textures differ: Poppies is suffused with love and longing; Remains is suffused with guilt and horror. Together they cover war's psychological damage from both sides of the separation.

Essay Point

Both Weir and Armitage show that war's most lasting damage is not physical but psychological — and both represent this through form. Poppies' enjambed, unresolved lines mirror the mother's fragmented, ongoing grief. Remains' shift to present tense in its final stanza ("sleep, and he's there in the morning") shows the trauma is not a memory but a present reality. Both poets refuse their speakers a neat ending, because the psychological reality of war doesn't offer one.

vs Kamikaze — Garland
Family fractured by warWhat goes unsaidLove vs duty
▼
Similarities

Both poems explore war's devastation through the lens of family relationships rather than the battlefield itself — neither poem describes combat directly. Both centre on the bond between parent and child, and both show that bond fractured by war's demands. Both also use silence and absence as central techniques: in Poppies, the mother suppresses her grief in a moment of parting ("steeled the softening"); in Kamikaze, the family enforces a deliberate silence toward the returning pilot — "they treated him as though he no longer existed." In both poems, what is not said carries more weight than what is.

Key Difference

The mother in Poppies suppresses her grief out of love — she does not want her son to carry her fear with him. The family in Kamikaze enforces silence as punishment — it is a deliberate act of cruelty dressed as social obligation. This reversal is critical: in Poppies, silence protects; in Kamikaze, silence destroys. The emotional register of the two poems is therefore very different — Poppies is suffused with tender, aching love; Kamikaze carries a colder, more disturbing grief. Garland's daughter eventually questions the silence ("till she too came to wonder"); Weir's mother cannot afford to question anything — she is still waiting.

Essay Point

Both Weir and Garland use the figure of a child departing from family to explore war's cost, but to opposite effect. Poppies asks: what does it cost to let someone go into danger? Kamikaze asks: what does it cost to refuse to let someone return? Both are about the damage done to families by military culture — one through the fear of loss, the other through the reality of a survival that no one will acknowledge. Both suggest that the domestic space, far from being a shelter from war, is itself a battlefield.

Beatrice Garland — 2011

Kamikaze

Duty vs. desire War & survival Identity Nature Family & shame
Context
Kamikaze pilots were Japanese fighters in WWII ordered to fly suicide missions — deliberately crashing their planes into Allied ships. "Kamikaze" means "divine wind" in Japanese, referring to the typhoons that destroyed Mongol invasion fleets in the 13th century. The missions were framed as the ultimate act of honour and national duty. A pilot who turned back faced permanent social ostracism — treated as if dead by his community and family. Beatrice Garland imagines such a pilot and the consequences of his survival through the voice of his daughter, who only later questions whether her father made the right choice.

Summary

A daughter narrates her father's story: a kamikaze pilot who turned back from his mission after seeing the sea and remembering his childhood fishing trips with his own father. Upon returning home, his family and community refused to acknowledge him — his wife and children were taught to behave as if he no longer existed. The poem ends ambiguously with the daughter "wondering" whether survival or death would have been the better choice.

Form & Structure

Third-person narration by the daughter — the distance of "he" and "his" reflects the emotional distance the family was forced to maintain. In the later stanzas, the pronoun shifts to "we" — the daughter implicates herself in his social death, admitting her own complicity. Long, flowing stanzas describing the sea and nature mirror the beauty that draws the pilot back to life. Shorter, clipped lines in the aftermath sections mirror the community's cold rejection. The poem ends with the word "wonder" — deliberately ambiguous between wonder (awe, appreciation of life) and wondering (doubt, uncertainty about the right choice). No resolution is offered.

Key Quotes & Analysis

"a samurai sword in his kimono"
The opening establishes the pilot firmly within Japanese cultural identity and the warrior code (Bushidō). The samurai sword is a symbol of honour, duty, and death — carrying it is the mark of a warrior who expects to die with dignity. Before any doubt enters the poem, Garland establishes what the pilot is supposed to be. The cultural specificity is important: this is not a generic soldier, but a man embedded in a particular code of honour.
"the little fishing boats strung out like bunting"
The simile of "bunting" — the colourful flags hung at celebrations — transforms the fishing boats into a symbol of civilian joy and festivity. Seen from the air, the world below is not a strategic target but something alive and celebratory. The contrast between his warplane above and these "little" boats below — ordinary, domestic, joyful — is what turns him back. Life, not death, is what the sea below him offers. "Little" is also poignant: the world of ordinary things is small but extraordinarily precious.
"he must have wondered / which had been the better way to die"
The central question of the poem, delivered with devastating precision. The choice is not between life and death — it is between two kinds of death: the physical death of the mission, or the social death of returning. "Must have wondered" — the daughter doesn't know; this is an imagined interiority. The word "wondered" here means genuine, unresolved uncertainty. The "better way to die" does not presuppose survival as the right choice, which is what makes the poem so unsettling.
"they treated him as though he no longer existed"
The community enacts a social death that mirrors the physical death he was supposed to have accepted. The grammar is important: "as though" marks a performance — they know he exists, but choose to act as if he doesn't. This deliberate performance of non-existence is, Garland implies, its own form of cruelty. The violence here is in the silence and the looking-through, not in any physical act.
"till she too came to wonder"
The final line's ambiguity is the poem's most important feature. "She too" — the daughter — only late in life begins to question. "Wonder" returns the poem's key word: wonder as awe (she wonders at the courage it took to choose life) or wonder as doubt (she wonders, as her father did, which choice was better). The poem refuses to resolve this. Crucially, the daughter admits she was complicit — "she too" came to wonder, implying she previously accepted her father's social erasure without questioning it.

⬆ Grade 9 Angle

The shifting pronouns are structurally crucial — track them. The poem begins at narrative distance ("he", "his"), then the daughter admits "we" — and this single pronoun shift is where the poem's emotional complexity concentrates. She is not simply telling her father's story; she is confessing her own role in it. A student who notices and analyses this shift will produce a genuinely perceptive reading.

Comparisons

vs The Prelude — Wordsworth
Nature's transformative powerSelf-discoveryPsychological shift
▼
Similarities

Both poems use an encounter with the natural world as the pivot point of a profound personal transformation. In The Prelude, the young Wordsworth steals a boat and rows out onto a lake, only to be confronted by a looming mountain that seems to stride after him — after which his sense of self is permanently altered. In Kamikaze, the pilot looks down at the sea from his plane and, seeing the "little fishing boats strung out like bunting" and the glittering fish below, turns back from his suicide mission. In both cases, nature intervenes in a moment of potential violence or hubris and redirects the individual's course entirely.

Key Difference

Nature's mode of transformation is opposite in the two poems. Wordsworth's mountain is threatening, huge, and imposing — it works through fear and a sense of the sublime, making the boy feel small and guilty. Garland's sea works through beauty and tenderness — the "tiger-striped mackerel" and the childhood memories the sea evokes are joyful, life-affirming things that pull the pilot back to the world of the living. Wordsworth is frightened away from transgression; the pilot is drawn back to love. The natural world is transformative in both, but through entirely opposite emotional registers — dread versus wonder.

Essay Point

Both Wordsworth and Garland use nature to argue that certain truths cannot be accessed through reason or ideology alone — they arrive through direct sensory experience of the natural world. Wordsworth's boy could not have been argued out of his guilt; the mountain had to show him. Garland's pilot could not have been argued out of his mission; the sea had to remind him of what life meant. Both poems suggest the natural world has a moral authority that human systems — whether childhood transgression or military duty — cannot override.

vs Charge of the Light Brigade — Tennyson
Military sacrificeDuty vs survivalGlorification vs questioning
▼
Similarities

Both poems deal with soldiers ordered toward almost certain death, and both explore the relationship between military duty and individual will. In both, the soldiers are embedded in a cultural code that demands obedience above survival. Tennyson's six hundred ride because "theirs not to reason why, / theirs but to do and die" — individual thought is subordinated entirely to the order. Kamikaze explores precisely the same subordination: the pilot carries a samurai sword, the symbol of a warrior code that requires him to die with honour. Both poems therefore examine what societies ask of soldiers in war.

Key Difference

The difference in attitude is fundamental. Tennyson glorifies the charge — the soldiers who follow suicidal orders are described as heroes, and the poem's energetic rhythm ("Cannon to right of them, / Cannon to left of them") sweeps the reader along with patriotic momentum. Garland deeply questions whether the pilot's expected sacrifice was right — and through the daughter's final ambiguity ("which had been the better way to die"), she refuses to endorse it. Tennyson asks readers to admire military obedience; Garland asks them to question it. This contrast in the poets' attitudes is the spine of any essay comparing them.

Essay Point

Tennyson and Garland are in direct ideological opposition. Tennyson's poem was written as public propaganda — to frame a catastrophic military blunder as heroic self-sacrifice. Garland's poem was written to ask whether that framing does justice to the human cost. The contrast between Tennyson's thundering dactylic rhythm (which mimics the charge) and Garland's quiet, elegiac free verse tells you everything about their different purposes: one is designed to carry you forward without thinking, the other to slow you down and make you doubt.

vs Exposure — Owen
Nature in warSoldiers controlled by forces beyond themFutility
▼
Similarities

Both poems place soldiers in the context of a powerful natural world, and in both poems that natural world is central to the poem's argument. Both also deal with soldiers who are controlled by forces entirely beyond their individual will — Owen's men are pinned down by weather, Garland's pilot is bound by military duty. Neither soldier has meaningful agency within the system that governs them. Both poems also refuse the reader any simple resolution: Owen's recurring line "But nothing happens" and Garland's final "wonder" both withhold closure and force the reader to sit with discomfort.

Key Difference

Nature functions in completely opposite ways in the two poems. In Exposure, nature is the enemy — the "merciless iced east winds that knive us" kill soldiers as effectively as bullets. Nature is indifferent, hostile, and lethal. In Kamikaze, nature is redemptive — the sea with its beautiful fish and memories of childhood fishing trips calls the pilot back to life. For Owen's soldiers, nature brings death; for Garland's pilot, nature is the reason to choose life. This reversal is the most important contrast: the natural world can be either the instrument of war's destruction or a reminder of everything worth living for.

Essay Point

Both Owen and Garland use detailed, specific natural imagery to make their arguments — Owen's winds "knive," "tug," and "paling flakes" accumulate into a landscape of suffering; Garland's sea gives us "tiger-striped mackerel" and shoals that "flatten and shimmer." The precision in both cases is not decorative but argumentative: Owen's nature is so specific in its cruelty that we cannot dismiss it as an abstraction; Garland's nature is so specific in its beauty that we understand immediately why the pilot turns back. Both poets use the physical world to make emotional arguments that abstract language could not.

vs Poppies — Weir
Family & warWhat goes unsaidLove vs duty
▼
Similarities

Both poems explore the damage war does to families and to parent-child bonds. Neither poem depicts combat — both focus on the domestic and relational cost of military culture. Both centre on a child-figure whose relationship to the family is fractured by war's demands: in Poppies the son departs and the mother waits; in Kamikaze the father returns and the family refuses him. Both also hinge on what is not said: Weir's mother suppresses her grief in a moment of parting, and Garland's family enforces deliberate silence as social punishment.

Key Difference

The silence in Poppies is an act of love — the mother holds herself together so as not to burden her son. The silence in Kamikaze is an act of cruelty — "they treated him as though he no longer existed." This reversal — same structural technique (silence), opposite emotional meaning — is the most productive essay point between these two poems. Weir's domestic imagery is tender and tactile; Garland's is absent — there is no domestic warmth in the pilot's homecoming because the home has been weaponised against him.

Essay Point

Weir and Garland both suggest that the domestic space is not a refuge from war but a site of war's damage. In Poppies, the home is haunted by imminent absence — every detail from the "blazer" to the "Sellotape" is saturated with the coming loss. In Kamikaze, the home becomes a place of enforced erasure. Together, the two poems argue that military culture colonises the domestic sphere — demanding that families sacrifice either their children or, in Kamikaze's case, their capacity for love.

Simon Armitage — 2008

Remains

PTSD & trauma Guilt Moral ambiguity of war Memory Psychological cost
Context
Simon Armitage wrote this poem as part of "The Not Dead" (2008), a Channel 4 documentary and poetry collection based on real testimonies from British soldiers suffering from PTSD after service in Iraq and Afghanistan. The poem is a dramatic monologue based directly on the account of a specific soldier who killed a man he believed to be a looter, and who was subsequently haunted by the act. PTSD (Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder) was receiving increasing public attention during the Iraq and Afghanistan wars; "Remains" is one of the most powerful literary treatments of the condition.

Summary

A soldier recounts how he and colleagues shot a man leaving a bank — "probably armed, possibly not." His colleagues move on; he cannot. The man's image haunts him: in the street, in his dreams, everywhere. He tries drugs and drink to forget, but the man remains — literally and psychologically — in his mind.

Form & Structure

Dramatic monologue — the soldier speaks directly, confessionally, in a casual colloquial voice that creates unease. The casual, conversational register (slang, euphemism, understatement) is the poem's most important technique: it shows a soldier who has been trained to dissociate from violence, and whose trauma emerges precisely because this dissociation has broken down. Enjambment throughout — the soldier's thoughts cannot be contained. The final stanza shifts to present tense, showing that PTSD lives not in the past but in the present moment. Repetition ("probably armed, possibly not" echoed; "end of story" appearing twice) enacts the obsessive, looping quality of traumatic memory.

Key Quotes & Analysis

"probably armed, possibly not"
The hedging adverbs — "probably," "possibly" — are the moral heart of the poem. The killing may not have been justified. The alliterative 'p' sounds give the phrase an almost throwaway rhythm, as if the soldier is recounting something trivial, which makes the moral weight hit harder. This line is the source of all the guilt that follows: the uncertainty never resolves. Armitage refuses to let the soldier (or the reader) off the hook.
"tosses his guts back into his body"
The visceral brutality of the image is intensified by the casualness of "tosses" — a word you'd use for a ball or a coin, not human remains. This dissociative language is how trauma and military desensitisation work: soldiers are trained to speak of violence in neutral terms. The graphic physicality forces the reader into the scene in a way that abstract language cannot. This is the poem's most deliberately disturbing line.
"His bloody life in my bloody hands"
Armitage's most controlled piece of wordplay. "Bloody" functions simultaneously as literal description (blood on his hands) and as a British expletive (a mild curse of frustration). The collapse of these two meanings mirrors the collapse of moral distance: the soldier cannot use his soldier-slang without it also being a confession of what he has done. The phrase refuses to let him speak about his guilt without being guilty.
"his blood-shadow stays on the street"
Armitage invents the compound noun "blood-shadow" — a new word for something that doesn't exist physically but is psychologically real to the soldier. A shadow follows you; it cannot be escaped. The shadow is "his" — belonging to the dead man, not the soldier — meaning the victim still possesses something even in death, namely a persistent, haunting presence. This is the soldier's PTSD made visible as a physical object.
"dug in behind enemy lines"
The military metaphor is repurposed to describe the soldier's own mind. "Dug in" means entrenched, established, impossible to dislodge — the dead man is now occupying territory inside the soldier. "Behind enemy lines" is a profound reversal: the enemy is no longer in a foreign country but inside him. The military language the soldier uses to describe his PTSD is the same language that caused it — he cannot escape the framework of conflict.
"sleep, and he's there in the morning"
The present tense is crucial — the trauma is not historical, it is happening now. The comma after "sleep" creates a tiny pause that enacts the hope of rest being immediately destroyed. The dead man is "there" — specific, persistent, unchanging. The line ends the poem (almost) without resolution: there is no cure, no forgetting, no end of story. The final phrase "End of story, except not really" earlier in the poem prepared us for exactly this — the story cannot end.

  3. "His bloody life in my bloody hands"double meaning of "bloody"; guilt as language
  4. "his blood-shadow stays on the street"invented compound; shadow = inescapable haunting

⬆ Grade 9 Angle

The tone is the technique. The soldier speaks in casual, colloquial language about extreme violence — "tosses," "end of story," "one of my mates." This is not carelessness; it is how trauma-induced dissociation sounds. Armitage makes the reader do the emotional work the soldier cannot. A Grade 9 student should note that the horror of the poem is produced by the gap between the tone and the content, not by explicit emotional language.

Comparisons

vs Ozymandias — Shelley
What remainsLegacy of violenceTime & memory
▼
Similarities

The title of Armitage's poem enters into direct dialogue with Shelley's. Shelley writes "Nothing beside remains" — great power leaves no trace. Armitage's title insists the opposite: everything remains. Both poems are therefore asking the same question — what endures after acts of human power or violence — and arriving at sharply different answers. Both also subvert grand narratives: Shelley undermines the tyrant's self-mythology; Armitage undermines the soldier's military language by making it reveal guilt rather than heroism.

Key Difference

For Shelley, the problem is that nothing lasts — the great tyrant's monument is dust. For Armitage, the problem is that everything lasts too long. The dead man's "blood-shadow stays on the street"; he is "dug in behind enemy lines" inside the soldier's mind, impossible to remove. Ozymandias would have envied this kind of persistence. This inversion of Shelley's central insight is Armitage's most powerful structural move: where Shelley shows erasure, Armitage shows inescapable haunting. The poems are mirror images of each other's argument.

Essay Point

Both poems use their final images to make their central arguments about what endures. Shelley closes with "the lone and level sands stretch far away" — infinite, indifferent emptiness. Armitage closes with "sleep, and he's there in the morning" — the dead man inescapably present in every waking moment. The contrast could not be more absolute. Where Shelley finds dark comfort in power's impermanence, Armitage finds no comfort anywhere — the past refuses to become past.

vs Exposure — Owen
Soldiers' sufferingOngoing traumaPresent tense horror
▼
Similarities

Both poems show the war experience as something that cannot be left behind. Owen's soldiers are reduced to near-death by waiting — "slowly our ghosts drag home" — the use of "ghosts" suggesting they are psychologically already dead while still living. Armitage's soldier is similarly haunted, carrying the dead man "dug in behind enemy lines" inside him wherever he goes. Both poems use the present tense at crucial moments to insist that the suffering is not historical but ongoing: Owen's recurring "But nothing happens" and Armitage's "he's there in the morning" both insist the war never truly ends for those who fought in it.

Key Difference

Owen's suffering is physical and collective — the poem is full of "we," and the damage is done to bodies by cold. Armitage's trauma is individual and psychological — it is one soldier, alone with a memory. Owen's men suffer from inaction — waiting is killing them. Armitage's soldier suffers from a single action — one moment that he cannot stop replaying. The nature of the damage is therefore different: Owen shows war as slow attrition, Armitage shows it as a single catastrophic moral rupture. Together, the two poems cover the full spectrum of how war destroys soldiers — from the body outward, and from the mind inward.

Essay Point

Owen and Armitage are writing nearly a century apart about different wars, but both reach the same conclusion: that soldiers are not prepared — by their training, their ideology, or their society — for what war actually does to them. Owen's men were told they were defending England's "payments of a day," but those abstractions give them no comfort in the freezing trench. Armitage's soldier was trained to act decisively, but the casual phrase "probably armed, possibly not" — uncertainty dressed as indifference — shows the military framework has no answer for moral doubt.

vs Bayonet Charge — Hughes
Stripping away ideologyRaw humanity under pressureBefore vs after
▼
Similarities

Both poems strip away the ideological veneer of war to expose what it actually does to individual human beings. Hughes shows a soldier in the moment of action — charging across no man's land — reduced to pure animal terror. Armitage shows a soldier in the aftermath — equally reduced, not by fear but by guilt. Both poets show that the things soldiers are told to believe in (patriotism, duty, the cause) entirely fail to support them in the actual experience of violence. Hughes's soldier drops "King, honour, human dignity, etcetera" as dead abstractions the moment bullets are flying; Armitage's soldier's casual slang reveals a similar collapse of meaning.

Key Difference

Hughes's poem is about the moment of combat — the present-tense, split-second reality of violence. The soldier hasn't yet done anything; he is mid-action. Armitage's poem is about the aftermath of a single act that has already happened. Hughes uses breathless, kinetic imagery — "stumbling", "sweating" — that moves at the pace of the charge. Armitage uses flat, retrospective language that has the quality of a statement being given under caution. Hughes shows us war in the body; Armitage shows us war in the mind, later, when the body has been taken home but the mind hasn't.

Essay Point

Hughes and Armitage can be read together as a before-and-after portrait of what war does. Hughes shows the ideology dissolving the moment combat begins; Armitage shows the soldier afterwards, trying and failing to reconstruct some stable sense of what he did and whether it was right. The gap between "probably armed, possibly not" — the casual uncertainty Armitage's soldier uses — and the absolute, terrified certainty of Hughes's charge reveals everything: in the moment, soldiers feel everything too intensely; afterwards, they feel nothing clearly enough.

vs War Photographer — Duffy
Moral weight of violenceHaunting imagesSociety's indifference
▼
Similarities

Both poems explore the psychological burden of being present at — and connected to — acts of violence in war. Both the soldier and the photographer cannot escape the images of the dead. Duffy's photographer develops photos in his darkroom and a face appears — "Something is happening. A stranger's features / faintly start to twist" — just as Armitage's soldier sees the dead man everywhere. Both figures carry their exposure to violence into civilian life and find that the gap between the world of war and the world at home is unbridgeable. Both poems also question society's relationship with war's violence: the newspaper readers who "reach for the Sunday papers" mirror the colleagues in Remains who simply move on.

Key Difference

The photographer witnesses but does not cause death; the soldier causes it. This is the fundamental moral distinction between the two poems. The photographer's guilt is about complicity — he records suffering for an audience that consumes it briefly and forgets. The soldier's guilt is about causation — "His bloody life in my bloody hands." Duffy's photographer can physically leave the darkroom; Armitage's soldier cannot leave his own mind. The soldier's trauma is therefore more inescapable: the photographer's images are printed and then can be filed away; the soldier's image cannot be developed and set aside.

Essay Point

Both Duffy and Armitage use their poems to ask what responsibility we bear for violence we are connected to. The photographer's culpability is institutional — he is part of a media machine that turns suffering into product. The soldier's culpability is personal — he pulled the trigger. But both poems ultimately ask the same question about the distance between action and consequence: how much do you have to be involved before it becomes yours? Armitage's answer — "His bloody life in my bloody hands" — is unambiguous. Duffy's is more complex, because the photographer's hands are technically clean.

vs Poppies — Weir
Psychological aftermathSuppressed emotionSoldier vs family
▼
Similarities

Both poems show the psychological aftermath of war — the fact that damage continues long after the fighting ends. Both use indirect language to convey extreme emotion: Weir's mother encodes grief in domestic details and suppressed actions ("steeled the softening"); Armitage's soldier encodes guilt in casual military slang ("probably armed, possibly not"). In both cases the intensity is produced by the gap between how the speaker talks and the weight of what they are carrying. Both poems also end without resolution — the mother is still listening for a voice that may never come; the soldier wakes each morning to find the dead man still there.

Key Difference

Poppies is about love — the mother's grief is an expression of the depth of her attachment. Remains is about guilt — the soldier's haunting is an expression of moral responsibility for harm he may have caused. The emotional register is therefore completely different: Poppies is tender, aching, lyrical; Remains is blunt, colloquial, and deliberately uncomfortable. Both show psychological damage, but the damage in Poppies is done to the speaker from outside (by separation and possible loss); in Remains it is done by the speaker to himself (through an action he cannot justify or forget).

Essay Point

Weir and Armitage together show the full human cost of war — from the home front and the soldier's perspective. The mother in Poppies can never know what her son experienced; the soldier in Remains carries something no civilian can share. Both poems are therefore also about isolation: the mother is isolated by not knowing; the soldier is isolated by knowing too well. War creates unbridgeable distances — between those who fight and those who wait — and both poems are located in the silence of those distances.

How the exam works, what examiners reward, and how to write at the top level

AO2 is almost half the marks. Identifying a technique is worth nothing on its own — the mark comes from explaining the effect on the reader. Never just name a technique; always follow it with "which creates the effect of..."

How to Structure Your Answer

The exam is one question, 45–55 minutes, worth ~30 marks. The named poem is printed on the paper. You choose the second poem yourself.

✗ The Wrong Approach

Writing all about Poem A, then all about Poem B at the end, with a rushed comparison. This is the most common mistake and it costs marks even if the analysis is good.

✓ The Right Approach

Weave comparison throughout. Introduce a point about Poem A, then immediately connect it to Poem B in the same paragraph. Examiners want to see comparison as a habit of thought, not a conclusion.

Paragraph Structure (for each body paragraph)

1. Make a comparative point — a statement about how both poems treat the theme similarly or differently.

2. Quote from Poem A + analyse language/form — focus on effect, not technique name.

3. Quote from Poem B + analyse language/form — link back to the comparative point.

4. Develop or complicate — introduce context, ambiguity, or a second reading. This is where Grade 8 becomes Grade 9.

Grade 9 Sentence Starters

Use these to force yourself to compare rather than describe:

  • Both poets use [X], however while [Poet A]'s [X] suggests..., [Poet B]'s implies...
  • The structural shift from [X] to [Y] reflects the poem's broader argument that...
  • The ambiguity of [word/phrase] allows the reader to interpret... which is significant because...
  • Whereas [Poet A] uses [technique] to convey..., [Poet B] achieves a similar effect through...
  • Significantly, both poets choose [technique] at this moment, suggesting that...
  • [Poet A]'s use of [technique] is complicated by [context], making the reader feel...
  • The contrast between [X] and [Y] is central to [Poet A]'s argument that...

What Separates Grade 7 from Grade 9

Grade 7 does this

Identifies techniques accurately. Makes solid points. Uses PEE or similar. Compares at the end. Mentions context correctly.

Grade 9 does this

Analyses effects, not just techniques. Notices what's absent or unexpected. Discusses form and structure as arguments, not decoration. Considers multiple interpretations. Integrates context naturally — it illuminates meaning, not just shows knowledge.

Grade 7 on a quote

"Shelley uses sibilance in 'lone and level sands' to create a sense of emptiness and vastness."

Grade 9 on the same quote

"The sibilance of 'lone and level sands' produces a slow, hissing emptiness that feels indifferent rather than mournful — nature does not mourn Ozymandias, it simply continues, which is more devastating than any explicit condemnation could be."

Context — How to Use It at Grade 9

Context should illuminate meaning, not decorate it. The test is: does this contextual point change how I read the poem? If the answer is no, leave it out.

✗ Context as decoration

Quick-Reference: Each Poem's Strongest Comparison Points

Ozymandias — Use for: power and its limits, the arrogance of rulers, nature's indifference, the gap between intended legacy and actual legacy, irony.

Checking Out Me History — Use for: identity and its construction, colonial power, the politics of knowledge, resistance through language and form.

Poppies — Use for: war's effect on families, suppressed grief, the perspective of those left behind, memory, the domestic vs. the military.

Kamikaze — Use for: duty vs. individual desire, nature's power to transform, shame and social exclusion, the cost of survival, family fracture, ambiguity of "right" choices.

Remains — Use for: PTSD and trauma, guilt, the moral ambiguity of war, how soldiers are damaged not just physically but psychologically, the inadequacy of language for extreme experience.
